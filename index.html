Learning, autonomy, and the transition beyond compulsory schooling

Educator, AI/ML graduate, and doctoral applicant exploring alternative educational systems grounded in human development.

My research examines the developmental thresholds at which learners become capable of transitioning from structured schooling into self-directed apprenticeship environments. I study how foundational literacy, reasoning ability, and social-emotional competencies interact to support autonomous learning, with the goal of informing educational systems that better align with human development and motivation.

Research Focus

I am interested in identifying when students have acquired the phonics, literacy, critical thinking, and self-regulatory capacities necessary to thrive in apprenticeship or dojo-style learning environments. My broader aim is to understand how educational systems might transition students from compulsory instruction into interest-driven mastery environments once core competencies are securely in place.

This work sits at the intersection of learning sciences, developmental psychology, educational systems design, and human-centered approaches to autonomy and motivation.

Current Research Questions

  • At what point do learners possess the literacy and reasoning skills needed to acquire knowledge independently?
  • Which social-emotional competencies best predict success in mentorship-based learning environments?
  • How can schools identify readiness for transition into apprenticeship-style models?
  • What supports are still needed in non-compulsory systems for students developing autonomy?

Why This Matters

Many students are capable of more autonomy than conventional schooling allows, while others need direct support in self-regulation, motivation, and social development before greater freedom becomes productive. I want to study that threshold carefully rather than assuming one fixed age or grade level works for everyone.
